Our friends ask us a lot of questions so we compilied some helpful answers to common questions that come up when getting into DeFi on PulseChain. Friends bugging you? - send 'em to Nacho!

99% of the time you forgot to add the token address in your wallet! So you do have the tokens but its just not showing up because you forgot to add that specific token address. Tokens must be added on each network separately. So don‚Äôt forget to add PLS, PLSX, WPLS, NACHO etc as tokens in your wallet on PulseChain. See our guide for how to add tokens to your wallet.
Also make sure you are looking in the right network on MetaMask. If you are looking for your NACHO on ETH network you won't find them. So switch your MetaMask wallet back to the PulseChain to see your PulseChain tokens!

Don‚Äôt worry! Now that you know how to bridge from the Ethereum network to the PulseChain network, getting back is the reverse process of our guide. One option is to swap everything to "DAI from Ethereum" on PulseChain and bridge the DAI back over to the Ethereum network. Then send your DAI to coinbase, and request a withdrawal straight to your bank account. This is not financial advice!

If there is a lot of volume on the network things can take awhile! Bridging in particular can take quite a while‚Ä¶ hours even. This is because quite a few mechanisms must be propagated throughout the blockchain validators to confirm all of the moving pieces. Rest assured its on the way and don‚Äôt forget to add the token address to your wallet so you will actually see the coins when they show up!

Sometimes a transaction doesn‚Äôt go through right away and is left in a pending state. If you try to make more transactions they will stack up and wait in order they were put in. This can sometimes be scary but there is a time period in which the transaction will time out if it does not go through successfully. So the best thing is to wait a couple hours and check back!

A hot wallet means that you are using it for a lot of different things, connecting to DEX‚Äôs for swapping, connecting to WEB3 apps and yield farming staking etc. This means the wallet could be vulnerable to being hacked if you are connecting it to a lot of different websites. If you mistakenly connect to a bad or fraudulent website you could end up inadvertently sending your crypto to scammers. So it's always good to keep your core bags (with the bulk of your funds) in a cold wallet! A cold wallet means that you don‚Äôt use it for everyday activities and really you only ever use it if you're going to move money back to your bank account. You would ideally buy a cheap computer and literally only set up a wallet on it and then disconnect from the internet and leave it in a secure place. It could also be a dedicated physical device designed for crypto such as a Trezor wallet or Ledger (which are hardware wallets). These are essentially the same as a computer but much smaller and similar to USB drives.

Pulse (PLS) is the native token on the PulseChain network. It is used as the currency for transaction fees to perform transactions on the block chain. Ether (ETH) is the native token on the Ethereum network used to pay for performing transactions on the Ethereum network. So PLS by itself is what is used to pay the fees for doing anything on PulseChain. PulseX (PLSX) is the token of the PulseChain Decentralized Exchange (DEX). PLSX can be staked to earn rewards and provide liquidity to the network. The PLSX token doesn't actually perform any function and it is a forked version of the UNI token of Uniswap. It differs in that it has what's called ‚Äúbuy and burn‚Äù meaning everything a transaction is made some of the fee gets burned or removed permanently, thus the total supply of PLSX goes down over time. PLSX will get more scarce over time. HEX is a separate token created to act like a Certificate of Deposit. Users can stake their HEX and earn rewards. Please educate yourself as much as possible and consider reading straight from each website.
  • Pulsechain.com
  • PulseX.com
  • Hex.com

A wrapped token is simply a copy of a token that is pegged to its value. The use case is to move tokens across different networks. To move PLS to the ethereum network, you must wrap it into WPLS. Your original PLS is now locked away and you are given the equivalent amount of WPLS which can be bridged over to other crypto networks.

On PulseChain if you bridge an Ethereum token over to PulseChain, its commonly called ‚ÄúToken from Ethereum‚Äù. For example, ETH from Ethereum or DAI from Ethereum. PulseChain forked the entire Ethereum network and gave everyone a copy of whatever they had in their wallets at the time of launch. This means that whatever you had on the Ethereum network is also now a new token on PulseChain Network. So there are actually two versions of ETH on PulseChain: ETH from Ethereum which is wrapped ETH bridged over, and then just simply ETH which is the copy of ETH that lives on PulseChain only. Think of it like eETH and pETH. Just always be sure to be using the correct token addresses when interacting with any of these as there are a lot of fakes and frauds out there.
